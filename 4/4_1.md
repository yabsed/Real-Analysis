# **Section 4.1: Limit of a Function** 

### 1. Definition of a Limit
**Context:** Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space, $E \subset X$, and $f: E \to \mathbb{R}$. Let $p$ be a **limit point** of $E$.

**Definition 4.1.1 ($\epsilon-\delta$ Definition):**
$\lim_{x \to p} f(x) = L$ if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that:
$$|f(x) - L| < \epsilon$$
for all $x \in E$ satisfying $0 < d(x, p) < \delta$.


* **Metric Space Extension:** If $f: E \to (Y, \rho)$, replace $|f(x)-L|$ with $\rho(f(x), L)$.
* **Neighborhood Notation:** $f(x) \in N_\epsilon(L)$ for all $x \in E \cap (N_\delta(p) \setminus \{p\})$.

**Key Remarks:**
1.  **Dependence:** $\delta$ depends on $\epsilon$, the function $f$, and often the point $p$.
2.  **Isolated Points:** If $p$ is not a limit point (isolated), the limit is meaningless.
3.  **Value at $p$:** It is **not** required that $p \in E$. Even if $p \in E$, $\lim_{x \to p} f(x)$ need not equal $f(p)$.

### 2. $\epsilon-\delta$ Proof Strategies (Examples)

#### A. Rational Functions (Algebraic Manipulation)
**Example 4.1.2(c):** $h(x) = \frac{\sqrt{x+1}-1}{x}$ for $x \in (-1, 0) \cup (0, \infty)$.
* **Claim:** $\lim_{x \to 0} h(x) = 1/2$.
* **Analysis:** Rationalize the numerator:
    $$|h(x) - \frac{1}{2}| = \left| \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+1}+1} - \frac{1}{2} \right| = \frac{|x|}{2(\sqrt{x+1}+1)^2}$$
    Since $(\sqrt{x+1}+1)^2 > 1$, we have $|h(x) - 1/2| < |x|/2$.
* **Choice:** Set $\delta = \min\{1, 2\epsilon\}$. Then $|h(x) - 1/2| < \epsilon$.

#### B. The Dirichlet Function (Nowhere Continuous)
**Example 4.1.2(d):** $f(x) = 1$ if $x \in \mathbb{Q}$, $0$ if $x \notin \mathbb{Q}$.
* **Claim:** $\lim_{x \to p} f(x)$ does not exist for any $p \in \mathbb{R}$.
* **Proof:** Fix $L$. Let $\epsilon = \max\{|L-1|, |L|\}$.
    * Density of $\mathbb{Q}$ implies $\exists x \in \mathbb{Q}$ near $p \implies |1-L| = \epsilon$.
    * Density of irrationals implies $\exists x \notin \mathbb{Q}$ near $p \implies |0-L| = \epsilon$.
    * Regardless of $\delta$, $|f(x)-L| \ge \epsilon$ is always possible. Thus, limit fails.

#### C. Modified Dirichlet Function (Limit exists at one point)
**Example 4.1.2(e):** $f(x) = 0$ if $x \in \mathbb{Q}$, $x$ if $x \notin \mathbb{Q}$.
* **At $x=0$:** Since $|f(x)| \le |x|$, choosing $\delta = \epsilon$ proves $\lim_{x \to 0} f(x) = 0$.
* **At $p \neq 0$:** Limit does not exist (similar argument to Dirichlet function).

#### D. Dependence of $\delta$ on $p$ (Uniformity issue)
**Example 4.1.2(f):** $f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0, 1)$. Show $\lim_{x \to p} (1/x) = 1/p$.
* **Inequality:** $|\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{p}| = \frac{|x-p|}{xp}$.
    Restricting $x > p/2$ implies $\frac{1}{xp} < \frac{2}{p^2}$.
* **Choice:** $\delta = \min\{p/2, p^2\epsilon/2\}$.
* **Key Insight:** As $p \to 0$, $p^2\epsilon/2 \to 0$. $\delta$ *must* shrink as $p$ approaches 0; it cannot be independent of $p$ for this domain.

#### E. Multivariable Limit
**Example 4.1.2(g):** $f(x, y) = \frac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$ on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus (0,0)$. Show limit at $(1, 2)$ is $2/5$.
* **Technique:** Algebraic factorization and Triangle Inequality.
    1.  **Common Denominator:**
        $$|f(x, y) - \frac{2}{5}| = \left| \frac{5xy - 2x^2 - 2y^2}{5(x^2+y^2)} \right|$$
    2.  **Numerator Decomposition:**
        The numerator is rewritten to isolate terms approaching zero, $(x-1)$ and $(y-2)$:
        $$5xy - 2x^2 - 2y^2 = (x-2y)(y-2) + (4y-2x)(x-1)$$
    3.  **Triangle Inequality ($|a+b| \le |a|+|b|$):**
        $$\le \frac{|x-2y||y-2|}{5(x^2+y^2)} + \frac{|4y-2x||x-1|}{5(x^2+y^2)} \le \frac{(|x|+2|y|)|y-2| + (4|y|+2|x|)|x-1|}{5(x^2+y^2)}$$
* **Bounding:** Restrict $(x,y)$ to the neighborhood $N_{1/2}(1,2)$.
    * This implies $1/2 < |x| < 3/2$ and $3/2 < |y| < 5/2$.
    * Using these values, we bound the coefficients (e.g., $5(x^2+y^2) > 25/2$) to find a constant $K = 26/25$.
    * $$|f(x, y) - \frac{2}{5}| < \frac{26}{25}(|y-2| + |x-1|)$$
* **Result:** Given $\epsilon$, choose $\delta < \min\{1/2, \frac{25}{52}\epsilon\}$.

### 3. Sequential Criterion for Limits
**Theorem 4.1.3:** $\lim_{x \to p} f(x) = L$ **if and only if** for every sequence $\{p_n\}$ in $E$ ($p_n \neq p$) with $p_n \to p$, the sequence $\{f(p_n)\} \to L$.

**Proof Technique: Constructing a Sequence ($\delta = 1/n$)**
The "If" direction ($\Leftarrow$) is often proven by contradiction (Contrapositive).
* **Assumption:** Suppose $\lim_{x \to p} f(x) \neq L$.
* **Negation of Limit:** There exists an $\epsilon_0 > 0$ such that for any $\delta > 0$, there is an $x$ with $0 < |x-p| < \delta$ but $|f(x) - L| \ge \epsilon_0$.
* **Construction:** For each $n \in \mathbb{N}$, choose $\delta = 1/n$.
    * We can find a point $p_n$ such that $0 < |p_n - p| < 1/n$ and $|f(p_n) - L| \ge \epsilon_0$.
* **Conclusion:** The sequence $\{p_n\}$ converges to $p$ (since $|p_n - p| < 1/n \to 0$), but $\{f(p_n)\}$ does not converge to $L$. This contradicts the hypothesis.

**Corollary 4.1.4 (Uniqueness):** If a limit exists, it is unique.

**Application: Disproving Existence of Limits**
To show a limit **does not exist**:
1.  Find a sequence $p_n \to p$ where $\{f(p_n)\}$ diverges.
2.  Find two sequences $p_n \to p, r_n \to p$ where $\lim f(p_n) \neq \lim f(r_n)$.

**Example 4.1.5(a):** $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ at $x \to 0$.


* Choose $p_n = \frac{2}{(2n+1)\pi}$.
* $f(p_n) = \sin((2n+1)\pi/2) = (-1)^n$.
* The sequence $(-1)^n$ does not converge. Thus, $\lim_{x \to 0} \sin(1/x)$ does not exist.

### 4. Limit Theorems
Let $\lim_{x \to p} f(x) = A$ and $\lim_{x \to p} g(x) = B$.
1.  **Algebra:**
    * $\lim (f+g) = A + B$
    * $\lim (fg) = AB$
    * $\lim (f/g) = A/B$ (provided $B \neq 0$).

    **Proof Detail for Quotient (Bounding away from 0):**
    To prove $\lim \frac{1}{g(x)} = \frac{1}{B}$, we must ensure $g(x)$ does not vanish near $p$.
    * **Specific Choice of $\epsilon$:** Set $\epsilon = \frac{|B|}{2}$.
    * **Logic:** Since $\lim g(x) = B$, $\exists \delta_1 > 0$ such that whenever $0 < |x-p| < \delta_1$, we have $|g(x) - B| < \frac{|B|}{2}$.
    * **Triangle Inequality:**
        $$|g(x)| = |B - (B - g(x))| \ge |B| - |g(x) - B| > |B| - \frac{|B|}{2} = \frac{|B|}{2}$$
    * **Result:** $|g(x)| > \frac{|B|}{2} > 0$. The denominator is strictly bounded away from zero by a specific positive constant in this neighborhood.

2.  **Boundedness Theorem (Thm 4.1.8):**
    If $g$ is bounded on $E$ ($|g(x)| \le M$) and $\lim_{x \to p} f(x) = 0$, then $\lim_{x \to p} f(x)g(x) = 0$.
    * *Example 4.1.10(c):* $\lim_{x \to 0} x \sin(1/x) = 0$. Since $|\sin(1/x)| \le 1$ is bounded and $x \to 0$, the product goes to 0.

3.  **Squeeze Theorem (Thm 4.1.9):**
    If $g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x)$ and $\lim g(x) = \lim h(x) = L$, then $\lim f(x) = L$.

### 5. Essential Trigonometric Limit
**Claim:** $\lim_{t \to 0} \frac{\sin t}{t} = 1$.
* **Geometric Proof:** Using unit circle areas (Triangle $OPQ$, Sector $OPR$, Triangle $ORS$).
    $$\text{Area}(\triangle OPQ) < \text{Area(Sector)} < \text{Area}(\triangle ORS)$$
    $$\frac{1}{2} \sin t \cos t < \frac{1}{2}t < \frac{1}{2} \tan t$$
* **Inequality:** $\cos t < \frac{\sin t}{t} < \frac{1}{\cos t}$.
* **Conclusion:** Since $\lim_{t \to 0} \cos t = 1$, by Squeeze Theorem, limit is 1.

### 6. Limits at Infinity
**Definition 4.1.11:**
Let domain of $f$ be unbounded above. $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$ if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists M \in \mathbb{R}$ such that:
$$|f(x) - L| < \epsilon$$
for all $x \in \text{Dom}(f) \cap (M, \infty)$.
*(Analogous definition exists for $x \to -\infty$ using $x < M$)*.

**Examples:**
1.  **Damped Sine:** $f(x) = \frac{\sin x}{x}$ on $(0, \infty)$.
    Since $|f(x)| \le 1/x$, choosing $M = 1/\epsilon$ proves limit is 0.
2.  **Oscillation at Infinity:** $f(x) = x \sin(\pi x)$.
    Choosing sequence $p_n = n + 1/2$ gives $f(p_n) = (-1)^n(n+1/2)$. This is unbounded; limit does not exist.
